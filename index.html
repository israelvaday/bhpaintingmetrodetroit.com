In depth

Questions about metro detroit painting across Metro Detroit

Practical answers from BH Painting Metro Detroit to help you compare painting scopes in Metro Detroit. Tap a question to expand.

Preparation depends on the surface and its condition. A clear scope may include washing, scraping loose coating, sanding glossy areas, filling minor holes, caulking selected gaps, and protecting adjacent finishes. Existing damage or repairs outside the painting scope should be identified before work begins.

Primer is commonly used on bare or repaired surfaces, difficult stains, major color changes, and areas where adhesion needs help. A previously painted surface in sound condition may only need spot primer. The written scope should identify where primer is planned and why.

Flat and matte finishes reduce reflection but can be less washable. Eggshell and satin add washability and highlight more surface variation. Semi-gloss is often considered for trim, doors, and moisture-prone areas. Product-specific performance matters, so sheen should be selected with the room and substrate in mind.

Coat count depends on the existing color, new color, paint product, application method, and surface porosity. Strong color changes may require primer or additional finish coats. We recommend defining the planned coating system and the expected finished appearance instead of relying on a blanket promise.

Review a physical sample in the actual room or exterior area at different times of day. Light, surrounding finishes, and sheen all affect perception. Record the manufacturer, color name, color number, product, and sheen before materials are ordered.

Temperature, surface moisture, rain, wind, and direct sun can change application and drying conditions. Exterior dates may move when conditions fall outside the selected product's requirements. A responsible schedule protects the coating system instead of promising work in unsuitable weather.

The closeout plan should cover removal of masking and project debris, orderly storage or removal of labeled touch-up paint, and a walkthrough against the written scope. Buyer's guide

How to compare painting estimates in Metro Detroit

A useful painting estimate identifies the surfaces, preparation, coating system, and exclusions. Use this checklist before approving a scope.

1. Compare the same surfaces. Confirm which walls, ceilings, trim, doors, cabinets, siding, decks, or fences are included and which are excluded.

2. Define surface preparation. Washing, scraping, sanding, filling, caulking, spot repairs, and protection should be described instead of grouped into a vague “prep” line.

3. Record primer and paint. Note the manufacturer, product line, color, sheen, and where primer is planned. Product selection can matter as much as coat count.

4. Clarify coats and coverage. Ask whether the quote defines a planned number of coats, a finished-appearance standard, or both, especially for dramatic color changes.

5. Plan color approval. Confirm who supplies samples, who gives final approval, and when colors must be locked to protect the schedule.

6. Review weather, access, and cleanup. Exterior work needs product-appropriate conditions. Interior and commercial work should address occupied spaces, daily reset, final cleanup, and the walkthrough.

Glossary

Painting terms, explained in plain language

These are the terms BH Painting Metro Detroit uses when discussing scope, products, and finish expectations.

Surface preparation — cleaning, scraping, sanding, filling, caulking, and spot repairs completed before paint is applied.

Primer — a preparatory coating selected for adhesion, stain blocking, color changes, or bare surfaces. Not every previously painted surface needs a full primer coat.

Sheen — how reflective the dried paint appears. Common choices include flat, matte, eggshell, satin, semi-gloss, and gloss.

Coat — one complete application of paint. Coverage depends on the existing color, new color, product, surface condition, and application method.

Cutting in — painting clean edges around ceilings, trim, corners, fixtures, and other areas a roller cannot reach precisely.

Dry time — when a coating can usually be recoated or lightly touched. Cure time is longer and describes when it reaches fuller hardness and durability.

Color sample — a test area or sample board reviewed under the room's actual daylight and artificial light before the final color is confirmed.

Punch list — the final walkthrough items addressed before cleanup and project closeout.
